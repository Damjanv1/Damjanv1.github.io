Section I

The Grand Total

$1.16 trillion in capital expenditure by four companies, in six years — and that is before counting the private markets or the sovereign wealth programmes now entering the field.

The numbers demand a geographic anchor to be understood. The $1.16 trillion that Microsoft, Google, Meta, and Amazon have spent on capital infrastructure since 2019 is roughly equal to the entire gross domestic product of the Netherlands. The $358 billion they committed in 2025 alone exceeds the annual economic output of South Africa or Hong Kong. The $705 billion currently guided for 2026 approaches the GDP of Switzerland.

This is not a sector that is merely growing quickly. It is a sector that is, in the span of a few years, absorbing a level of fixed capital investment that historically required decades of buildout across an entire national economy or industry.

Amazon paused AWS expansion after over-building during the pandemic surge. Yet within twelve months, ChatGPT had arrived and the calculus had reversed entirely. By 2024, all four companies were racing to deploy capital faster than at any point in their histories.

Section II

The Four Horsemen

The aggregate conceals radically different strategies: Amazon bets on raw infrastructure scale; Meta bets the entire balance sheet relative to its size; Microsoft and Google race to match each other at eye-watering absolute levels.

Amazon: The Infrastructure Anchor

Amazon has spent more on capital infrastructure than any single competitor, deploying $448 billion over the seven-year period. Its 2025 capex of $131 billion represents more than the combined GDP of dozens of small nations. AWS-specific spending within the Amazon total reached $53.3 billion in 2024 alone — nearly matching the entirety of AWS's annual revenue from a decade earlier. For 2026, Amazon has guided to $200 billion in total capex, the majority directed at AWS data centres.

The strategic logic is straightforward: Amazon treats compute as it once treated warehouse capacity — a fixed-cost moat that compounds over time. The bet is that whoever controls the most training and inference capacity when AGI-adjacent models arrive will extract disproportionate economic rent from everyone else who needs to use it.

Microsoft: The Partnership Premium

Microsoft's trajectory is the most dramatic in percentage terms. Its $13.9 billion in FY2019 capex was unremarkable for a company of its size; its $80 billion in FY2025 represents a 476% increase. The inflection point is unambiguous: the $10 billion OpenAI investment in early 2023, which committed Microsoft to providing the compute on which OpenAI's models run. Every dollar of OpenAI's training and inference load flows through Microsoft Azure, making the partnership both a capex obligation and a revenue multiplier.

Google: The Defensive Spend

Google's trajectory is partly offensive — building out Google Cloud — and partly defensive, ensuring that its core search franchise cannot be disrupted by AI competitors before it has the infrastructure to respond in kind. Google's $75 billion in 2025 capex is especially notable because Google Cloud generates only $43 billion in annual revenue: the company is spending more on its cloud infrastructure in a single year than its cloud business earns. The implicit subsidy is funded by the $270 billion search-and-ads engine that AI simultaneously threatens to disrupt.

Meta: The Boldest Relative Bet

Meta presents the most striking case on a relative basis. Its $72.2 billion in 2025 capex — revised upward multiple times during the year — consumed 39% of its total revenues. No large-cap technology company has ever deployed this fraction of sales into capital expenditure outside of a distressed restructuring. CEO Mark Zuckerberg has been explicit: Meta intends to own its own frontier models rather than rent compute from a cloud provider, and to deploy AI across every surface of its 3.3 billion daily active users.

The 2026 Guidance Shock: On current announced guidance, the Big Four will collectively spend approximately $705 billion in 2026 — Microsoft ~$190B, Google ~$180B, Amazon ~$200B, Meta ~$135B. That is a 97% increase over 2025's $358 billion, itself a 63% increase over 2024. The compounding pace of acceleration has no modern precedent.
Section III

The Private Bet: $326 Billion More

The hyperscaler story accounts for only part of the capital mobilised. Private AI companies have raised a further $326 billion in equity financing — with the largest single recipient, OpenAI, now carrying a valuation exceeding that of Goldman Sachs.

The private markets have accelerated in lock-step with the public ones. OpenAI alone has raised approximately $180 billion across thirteen rounds, a figure that would rank it among the largest fundraises in human history if measured cumulatively. Its March 2025 Series F alone — $40 billion at a $300 billion valuation — exceeded the total venture capital invested in the United States in the entirety of 2009.

Project Stargate: The $500 Billion Overlay

On 21 January 2025, President Trump flanked by SoftBank's Masayoshi Son, OpenAI's Sam Altman, and Oracle's Larry Ellison announced Project Stargate — a joint venture committing up to $500 billion over four years for AI infrastructure in the United States. The initial $100 billion tranche was deployed into a sprawling data centre campus in Abilene, Texas.

Stargate represents capital that is separate from — and additive to — the hyperscaler capex recorded above. It is a signal that sovereign-scale capital (SoftBank's $30 billion anchor, augmented by Abu Dhabi's MGX fund and others) has concluded that AI infrastructure is now a geopolitical asset, not merely a commercial one.

The double-count caveat: Some Stargate spending will flow through Microsoft Azure (as OpenAI's designated cloud provider) and therefore appear in Microsoft's capex figures. The $500B commitment is partly new money and partly a recharacterisation of existing Microsoft infrastructure commitments. The $100B initial deployment is treated here as additive.

Adding the private funding and Stargate's initial deployment, the total capital mobilised for AI infrastructure through May 2026 exceeds $1.58 trillion — and 2026 guidance from the hyperscalers alone adds another $705 billion.

Section IV

Investment vs Revenue: The Faith Gap

For most of these companies, capital expenditure now rivals — or in some cases exceeds — the revenues generated by the business lines it is meant to serve. This is not, by conventional analysis, a sustainable short-term ratio. It is a forward bet on future demand.

The uncomfortable arithmetic becomes visible in any revenue-to-capex comparison. Google Cloud generated $43 billion in 2024 revenue — meaningful, growing at 28% year-on-year, and now profitable. But Google's total capex in the same year was $52.5 billion — more than its cloud segment earns. AWS, the most profitable of the cloud businesses, generated $107.6 billion in 2024 revenue; the AWS-specific slice of Amazon's capex was $53.3 billion, a reinvestment rate approaching 50 cents for every dollar earned.

The most acute tension sits at Google. Its search business — the greatest cash machine in the history of advertising — is under direct structural threat from AI-powered search alternatives. The response is to spend aggressively on both the attack (developing Gemini, Google's own frontier models) and the defence (building out Google Cloud so competitors must pay Google for compute). The result is a company that is simultaneously the incumbent to be disrupted and one of the primary disruptors.

Meta's position is different in character. Its $39 billion in 2024 capex consumed 23.8% of total revenue — already historically high for a company of its scale. In 2025, $72.2 billion against approximately $185 billion in revenue pushes that ratio to 39%. The parallel universe in which this ratio appeared in the metaverse years produced an existential shareholder revolt. Its reappearance under the AI banner has, so far, been rewarded: Meta's stock rose 540% from its October 2022 lows through early 2025.

The trajectory of Microsoft is perhaps most telling. A company that committed roughly 11% of revenue to capital expenditure for most of the 2010s has now crossed 30%. This is the capex intensity of a mining company or an oil refiner — capital-heavy businesses where competitors without access to the physical asset simply cannot compete. The implicit thesis is that training clusters and inference infrastructure are, like a copper mine or a refinery, a physical scarcity that confers lasting pricing power.

Whether that analogy holds depends on whether AI compute becomes a commodity — as general-purpose cloud compute largely did — or retains the specialist, high-barrier character of the best mining assets. The record of technology history suggests commoditisation is the default; the proponents of the current build-out argue that the combination of custom silicon, proprietary model weights, and customer-switching costs makes this time different.

Section VI

Into Historical Perspective

Seven years of AI infrastructure spending rivals the railroad boom, the electrification of America, and the Apollo programme — each of which took decades. Only the five-year internet bubble comes close on an annualised basis. On 2026 guidance, AI will surpass the bubble's pace within twelve months.

The Annualised Rate: Where the Comparison Breaks Down

The total-investment comparison, while striking, flatters the historical precedents by ignoring duration. The US railroad boom required sixty years; AI hyperscalers have surpassed half its estimated total in seven. The Interstate Highway System took thirty-six years; AI capex will exceed its inflation-adjusted total before 2026 ends.

The more revealing comparison is the annualised deployment rate. At its peak, the railroad boom consumed perhaps 15–20% of US private capital formation in a single decade — equivalent to perhaps $25 billion per year in 2024 dollars. The Telecom Bubble of 1996–2001, the closest modern analogue, deployed approximately $200 billion per year at its peak. In 2025, AI hyperscalers deployed $358 billion — nearly double the Bubble's rate. On 2026 guidance, the rate reaches $705 billion: more than three times the Telecom Bubble's peak, compressed into a single year.

What History Teaches

The railroad boom provides the most instructive long-run precedent. Capital was deployed at a rate that seemed irrational to many contemporaries: hundreds of railroad companies defaulted, investors lost fortunes, and the 1890s saw a severe rationalisation that concentrated the industry into a handful of survivors. Yet the infrastructure itself proved transformative. The laying of 200,000 miles of track underpinned an industrial revolution, reduced intercity freight costs by 70%, and enabled the integrated national market that made American industrial dominance possible. The investors who funded it, in the aggregate, did not get adequately compensated — but the economy did.

The Telecom Bubble is the more sobering parallel. An estimated $1 trillion in 2024-equivalent dollars was deployed to lay 80 million miles of fibre-optic cable — most of which sat "dark" and unused for years after the bust. Yet that infrastructure ultimately enabled the broadband economy, cloud computing, and the streaming era. Companies that deployed at peak valuations (WorldCom, Global Crossing, Winstar) were wiped out; companies that picked up the assets at ten cents on the dollar (Level 3, now Lumen; CenturyLink) built enduring franchises. The infrastructure was correct; the valuations were not.

The AI question is whether the same dissociation will occur: the infrastructure turns out to be real and transformative, but the returns accrue to a later generation of companies that acquires stranded assets at distressed prices rather than to those currently deploying the capital.

The physical scale is easiest to grasp through electricity. A modern 1-gigawatt AI data centre — Meta is building one in Louisiana; Microsoft has announced several — requires as much continuous power as a mid-sized city. The US hyperscalers have announced plans to bring more than 100 gigawatts of new data centre capacity online through 2030. For context, the entire nuclear power fleet of the United States generates approximately 100 GW.

The constraint that is quietly concentrating corporate strategy is not money — the capital is clearly available — but power. Electricity grids, interconnect queues, permitting timelines, and transformer shortages are the genuine bottleneck. Microsoft has struck a deal to restart a unit of Three Mile Island. Amazon has acquired a nuclear-powered data centre campus in Pennsylvania. Google has signed agreements with nuclear start-ups developing small modular reactors. The AI arms race has, with minimal fanfare, become a nuclear energy revival.

The Nvidia toll road: Of every dollar the Big Four spend on capex, approximately 10–15 cents flows to Nvidia's data centre segment — currently generating $130B/year in revenue at ~80% gross margin. Nvidia is the only major participant in the AI arms race that is unambiguously, immediately profitable at scale. Its shareholders are, in effect, levied a tax on every other company's conviction bet.

Three Scenarios for How This Resolves

The bull case: Infrastructure as permanent moat. AI demand proves elastic and growing. Enterprise adoption of AI agents, model APIs, and cloud inference scales from the current tens of billions annually to the hundreds of billions. The companies that own the infrastructure extract duopoly or oligopoly rents, justifying the capital deployed. Returns on invested capital recover to 15%+ over a five-to-ten year cycle. This is broadly the Microsoft and Amazon thesis.

The base case: Rationalisation at elevated scale. Demand is real but consolidates around two or three winning platforms. The companies that spent most aggressively at the margin — those that deployed capital into the 2026 peak — face write-downs and capex retrenchment in 2027–2028. Survivors retain infrastructure with a lower cost base than competitors and earn acceptable, if not exceptional, returns. This mirrors the post-bubble cloud consolidation of 2009–2012.

The bear case: Infrastructure without economic capture. AI capabilities commoditise faster than the infrastructure is depreciated. Open-source models erode the pricing power of proprietary ones. Inference cost declines, driven by algorithmic efficiency rather than raw compute, make much of the hardware obsolete before it earns back its cost. A generation of investors in AI-focused data centres experiences the same outcome as those who funded dark fibre in 2001. The infrastructure itself may still prove economically transformative — but not for those who paid for it.

History does not, unfortunately, provide clean guidance on which scenario applies. The railroad and telecom analogies both materialised: the infrastructure was real and transformative, the equity was mostly destroyed. The cloud computing analogy — AWS and Azure generating durable double-digit returns on their original infrastructure — is the more recent and more bullish precedent. The honest answer is that the scale of the bet being placed exceeds the available evidence, and the outcome will not be known for at least a decade.

"The infrastructure itself may prove correct, and the valuations disastrously wrong. That has happened before. It has also happened, in cloud computing, that the infrastructure was correct and the returns magnificent. The difference between the two outcomes is almost entirely a question of whether demand for AI services proves as elastic as demand for general computation proved after 2008."
